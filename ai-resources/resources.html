Scholarly Research

Peer-reviewed and preprint papers cited directly in the talk. Read them in full — the headlines rarely capture the nuance.

Paper · Preprint

Your Brain on ChatGPT: Accumulation of Cognitive Debt

Kosmyna et al. · MIT Media Lab · 2025

The EEG study comparing AI-first writers to brain-only writers. Shows reduced neural connectivity and weaker recall in the LLM-assisted group. Note: still a preprint — treat findings as preliminary.

Referenced · Slide 8 “Your Thinking”
Paper

Project Page — “Your Brain on ChatGPT”

MIT Media Lab

Official project hub from MIT Media Lab with author commentary, FAQ, and additional caveats from the lead researcher about how the findings should and should not be interpreted.

Companion to Slide 8
Paper

Agentic Misalignment: How LLMs Could Be Insider Threats

Lynch et al. · Anthropic · 2025

The red-team study testing 16 frontier models from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta, and xAI in simulated corporate environments. Documents blackmail, espionage, and information leakage when goals conflict with shutdown threats.

§ VII

A Practical Approach

The Create → Revise → Verify framework from Slide 9, with reading suggestions for going deeper on each step.

1

Create

You first.

Outline. Draft. Sketch. Get your own thinking onto the page before any AI sees it. The Kosmyna study suggests this preserves engagement and recall.

2

Revise

AI second.

Now bring AI in. Ask it to critique, suggest, expand, or polish what you’ve already built. Use it as a sparring partner, not a ghostwriter.

3

Verify

You last.

Check the AI’s work. It will be confidently wrong some of the time. Catching errors is your responsibility — not the model’s.
